How to Read This Dashboard ▼

Market makers who sell options hedge their own exposure by trading the underlying. The direction of that hedging flow depends on whether dealers are net long or net short gamma overall — that's what Net GEX approximates.

Positive Net GEX (dealers long gamma)

Dealer hedging tends to be counter-trend: they sell into rallies and buy dips. This dampens realized volatility and can make price feel "pinned" or mean-reverting near large open-interest strikes.

Negative Net GEX (dealers short gamma)

Dealer hedging tends to be trend-following: they buy as price rises and sell as it falls. This can amplify moves and produce sharper, more volatile swings — often seen during selloffs.

The Zero-Gamma Flip card is the strike where cumulative gamma crosses from positive to negative. Price above that level is generally the calmer, positive-gamma regime; price below it is the choppier, negative-gamma regime — some traders watch for the underlying crossing this level as a cue that the character of price action may be changing.

The chart and table below show where gamma concentrates by strike. Strikes with unusually large call or put GEX act like "walls": heavy call gamma above spot can act as resistance/a magnet into expiration (pinning), and heavy put gamma below spot can act as support — through the same dealer-hedging mechanics. These effects are strongest close to expiration, especially for SPY's 0DTE contracts.

The sign convention here (calls positive, puts negative) is a standard public-domain assumption about dealer positioning, not verified fact — actual market-maker books aren't public, so treat direction as an estimate, not certainty. This is a single snapshot metric, not predictive on its own, and works best combined with other context (implied/realized vol, technical levels, event calendars). This dashboard is for informational purposes only and is not financial advice; options trading carries substantial risk, including rapid and total loss of premium.
